How does this year's rainfall in SoCal stack up?

Produced by Aaron Mendelson | Updated Novemeber 16, 2016

As Southern California moves into another year of drought, every drop of rain helps. But just how much rain is falling this year? And, since it's a La Niña year, will we see below normal rainfall? To answer these questions, KPCC is working with the Scripps Institute of Oceanography to put this winter's precipitation in context.

The graph below reflects rainfall data from 20 rain gauges around the Los Angeles Basin. These gauges have detailed records stretching back decades, and give a picture of how much rain is fallilng across the region. This year's rainfall is reflected in the the blue line.

The dark gray line shows the median, or typical year. The light gray line displays the median for five previous strong La Niñas: 1974, 1976, 1989, 1999 and 2000. The graph shows the six-month period of the 'water year' when California receives most of its rain. A number above 100 means it's been an above-average year for rain.

Sources: Data via David Pierce of the Scripps Institution of Oceanography at UC San Deigo, California-Nevada Climate Applications Program and Center for Western Weather and Water Extremes. The daily data is available at this link. The percents reflect weighted rainfall totals, which are explained here. The most recent data is revised as higher quality data becomes available.
