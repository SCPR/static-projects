How does this year's rainfall in SoCal stack up?

Produced by Aaron Mendelson | Published October 18, 2017

How much rain has Southern California really received this year? Did that last storm make a difference in the water picture? Will a forecasted weak La Niña come to pass? To answer these questions, KPCC is working with the Scripps Institute of Oceanography to put our precipitation in context.

The graph below reflects rainfall data from 20 rain gauges around the Los Angeles Basin. These gauges have detailed records stretching back decades, and give a picture of how much rain is falling across the region. This year's rainfall is reflected in the the blue line.

The dark gray line shows the median, or typical year. The light gray line displays the median for five previous strong La Niñas: 1974, 1976, 1989, 1999 and 2000. The graph shows the full year, although Southern California typically receives most of its precipitation during the first six months of the water year. A number above 100 means it's been an above-average year for rain.

Sources: Data via David Pierce of the Scripps Institution of Oceanography at UC San Deigo, California-Nevada Climate Applications Program and Center for Western Weather and Water Extremes. The daily data is available at this link. The percents reflect weighted rainfall totals, which are explained here. The most recent data is revised as higher quality data becomes available; data updates frequently.
