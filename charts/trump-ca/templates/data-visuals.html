Trump's absence from the Golden State has been a notable break from the travel patterns of his predecessors. George W. Bush visited the state after 129 days. And Barack Obama visited California after just 57 days in office. In fact, every president since at least Jimmy Carter has found time to visit California in his first six months, the New York Times reports. Every president, that is, except Trump.

Many of the states the president visited before his upcoming trip to California voted for him in the 2016 election. And while California went for Hillary Clinton, 4.5 million Californians cast their vote for Trump. That's more votes than he received in Iowa, West Virginia and Arizona combined, three states in which Trump has held rallies.

GOP political consultant Mike Madrid told KPCC that California has historically been a "destination point" for presidents. But the country's increasing polarization and California's continued shift to the left have transformed that landscape. Instead of visiting the Golden State, Madrid sees Trump as "doubling down" on red states.

Why do presidents come to California?

Presidents typically make trips for a few reasons: to rally support, raise money and provide comfort and leadership after natural disasters and tragedies.

While Trump could draw an enthusiastic crowd in Fresno or Bakersfield, he's likely to draw protesters in deep-blue areas. And a visit by an unpopular president may harm election and re-election prospects for Republicans in areas that Clinton carried, including in several competitive Orange County congressional races.

Trump's March travel plans include a viewing of border wall prototypes and a fundraiser in Los Angeles. Mike Pence made a similar trip in October 2017, raising money across the state.

Some in the state were surprised that Trump didn't visit after the 2017 fires in Northern California that killed more than 40. "He was very quick in his responses to hurricanes in red states," UC San Diego political science professor Thad Kousser said. "Even though California may not need a presidential visit to recover from this, it certainly stands out when you see the president not responding to what's one of the biggest natural disasters in California history."

Madrid counters that Trump quickly approved federal financial relief for the state after the fires and the Oroville Dam collapse. There's little political benefit to Trump visiting California after crises, he said, or at any point. "It's got to be probably eleventh on a list of 10 priorities for him."

Madrid adds that the lack of visits has produced at least one silver lining: fewer traffic jams from presidential motorcades.

Policy-wise, Trump has staked out positions at odds with Gov. Jerry Brown and other California politicians, on everything from health care to immigration to climate change. Trump even called the state "out of control" in a February 2017 interview. And the feeling seems to be mutual, with California politicians declaring what Kousser describes as a "policy war" on the president.

Recently, Trump even floated removing U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents from California.
