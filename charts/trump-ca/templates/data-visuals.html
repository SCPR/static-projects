Trump's absence from the Golden State has been a break from the travel patterns of his predecessors. George W. Bush visited the state after 130 days. And Barack Obama visited California after just 58 days in office.

Many of the states the President has visited voted for him in the 2016 election. And while California went for Clinton, more than 3 million Californians cast their vote for Trump. That's more votes than he received in Iowa, West Virginia and Arizona, three states that Trump has help rallies in â€” combined.

GOP political consultant Mike Madrid said California has historically been a "destination point" for presidents. But the country's increasing polarization and California's continued shift to the left has changed the landscape. Instead of visiting the Golden State, Madrid said Trump seems to be "doubling down" on red states.

Does it matter that Trump hasn't visited?

"Don't hold your breath waiting for the presidential visit," said UC San Diego political science professor Thad Kousser. Presidents typically make trips for a few reasons: to rally support, raise money, and provide comfort and leadership after disasters and tragedies.

While Trump could draw an enthusiastic crowd in Fresno or Bakersfield, Kousser said, he's unlikely to hold a rally in deep-blue areas. And a visit by an unpopular president may harm re-election prospects for congressional Republicans representing areas that Hillary Clinton carried.

A fundraising swing is more plausible. While Trump hasn't made one yet, Mike Pence raised money across the state in October.

Some in the state were surprised that Trump didn't visit after the fires in Northern California that killed more than 40. "He was very quick in his responses to hurricanes in red states," Kousser said. "Even though California may not need a presidential visit to recover from this, it certainly stands out when you see the president not responding to what's one of the biggest natural disasters in California history."

Madrid counters that Trump quickly approved federal financial relief for the state after the fires and the Oroville Dam collapse. He says there's little political benefit to Trump visiting after crises, or at any point. "It's got to be eleventh on a list of ten priorities for him," he said.

Madrid said the lack of visits has produced at least one silver lining: fewer traffic jams from presidential motorcades.

Policy-wise, Trump has staked out positions at odds with Gov. Jerry Brown and other California politicans, on everything from health care to immigration to climate change. Trump even called the state "out of control" in a Feburary interview. And the feeling seems to be mutual, with California politicans declaring what Kousser describes as a "policy war" on the president.
