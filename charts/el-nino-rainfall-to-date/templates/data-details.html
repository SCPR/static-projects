El Niño

How does this year's rainfall in SoCal stack up?

Produced by Aaron Mendelson | Updated March 31, 2016

How much rain has El Niño brought to Los Angeles? It can be a tough question to answer, which is why KPCC is working with the Scripps Institute of Oceanography to put this year's "monster" weather event in context. A version of this chart covering the full October to September water year is available here.

The graph below reflects rainfall data from 20 rain gauges in greater Southern California. These gauges are geographically diverse, and have detailed records stretching back decades. They help give a clearer picture of how much rain is fallilng across the region this year: that's the orange line.

The gray line shows the median, or typical year. The blue line displays the median for five previous strong El Niños: 1958, 1966, 1973, 1983 and 1998. The graph shows the six-month period of the 'water year' when California receives most of its rain. A number above 100 means it's been an above-average year for rain.

Sources: Data via David Pierce of the Scripps Institution of Oceanography at UC San Deigo, California-Nevada Climate Applications Program and Center for Western Weather and Water Extremes. The daily data is available at this link. The percents reflect weighted rainfall totals, which are explained here. The most recent data is revised as higher quality data becomes available.
