20 things to know about Hotel California

Produced by Kevin Ferguson & Chris Keller | Published May 15, 2014

It's 35 seconds longer than Queen's Bohemian Rhapsody and has been covered hundreds of times, including musicians like Nancy Sinatra, Frank Ocean, Marilyn Manson… and remember William Hung? From American Idol?

Hotel California is the biggest hit single by the Eagles and it's literally everywhere.

According to Nielsen Soundscan Hotel California is played on American radio once every 11 minutes — that's about 131 times a day. And the average classic rock radio listener will hear it over 1500 times in their life—assuming they were born after the song came out.

And now yet another version has been recorded by former Eagle and one of the song's original writers Don Felder to drum up publicity for his upcoming solo tour.

Here are 20 other facts and tidbits we've been able to uncover about that place you can "checkout" from "but never leave."

What's the likelihood Hotel California is being played on the radio right now?

Good question

... and difficult to discern, but Anna Loynes with Scoop Marketing was able to provide some data that might lend an insight:

  • Looking at airplay data from April 28, 2014, "Hotel California" was played an average of once every 11 minutes or so on all of the U.S. radio stations monitored by Nielsen BDS.
  • Over a four-week period ending April 28, 2014, Classic Rock and Classic Hits stations monitored by Nielsen BDS played "Hotel California" 2,209 times, making it the 9th most-played song on those two formats. The most-played song during that period was Boston's "More Than A Feeling" with 2,700 plays.
  • Over a four-week period ending April 28, 2014, KRTH in Los Angeles played “Hotel California” 99 times, the most out of all of the U.S. radio stations monitored by Nielsen BDS. "Hotel California" was the 2nd most-played song over that time period (tied with America's "Sister Golden Hair").
  • Of the 10 US stations monitored by Nielsen BDS that played "Hotel California" the most over a four-week period ending April 28, 2014, four of those stations were in California.
